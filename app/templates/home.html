EU Budget 2014-2019 Dashboard

Visualizing expenditures of the European Union

This dashboard serves to display the European Union (EU) expenditure records between 2014 and 2019. It serves as a tool to establish transparency for EU citizens, by easily allowing everyone to track EU priorities, development ambitions and election promises. The spending categories can be distinguished into 5 focus areas, which you can explore by clicking the blue buttons.

Instructions

  1. Select up to three (3) EU member states using the map
  2. Click through the budget categories displayed in the sunburst chart
  3. Drag the time slider to switch between budgets of different years
  4. Select one budget category and compare its development over time across countries using the bar chart
  5. Press the Reset button to reset all choices
  6. To learn more about the spending categories and EU Budget, select the blue buttons on the bottom of the page

Spending Categories

Smart & Inclusive Growth

Since foresting propserity is one of the core values of the EU, this category is one of the biggest expenditures in the budget. It includes funding for programs focusing on education, research, innovation as well as transportation, telecommunication and energy infrastructure for economic, social and terretorial cohesion. Some of the biggest projects in 2019 included:

  • CSF - Common Strategic Framework Research and Innovation
  • Horizon 2020 - The Framework Programme for Research and Innovation
  • Erasmus+ - The Union Programme for Education, Training and Sport
  • EGNOS and GALILEO - Implementation and exploitation of European satellite navigation systems
  • Copernicus - European Earth Observation Programme
  • CEF - Connecting Europe Facility
Sustainable Growth & Natural Resources

Another large budget retainer is reserved for investments in the agriculture and natural resource maintainance. This is due to the fact that the majority of agricultural initiatives are funded through the common EU budget, thereby replacing national expenditure to a large extend. Some of the largest projects in 2019 included:

  • EAGF - European Agricultural Guarantee Fund - Market related expenditure and direct payments
  • EAFRD - European Agricultural Fund for Rural Development
  • EMFF - European Maritime and Fisheries Fund
  • SFAs - Sustainable Fisheries Partnership Agreements and compulsory contributions to Regional Fisheries
  • Life - Programme for the Environment and Climate Action

Security & Citizenship

Ensuring the safety and well-being of citizens is another key priority defined in the EU budget. This bucket covers home affairs, border protection, immigration and asylum policy, public health, consumer protection, culture, youth, civil protection and dialogue with citizens It mainly funds decentralized EU agencies that enforce EU policy, such as Frontex, ENISA and Europol. Some of the largest expenditure categories include:

  • Decentralised agencies
  • Asylum, Migration and Integration Fund
  • Internal Security Fund
  • Food and Feed
  • Creative Europe
Global Europe

Another portion of the budget is reserved to cover all external action regarding foreign policy by the EU, such as development assistance and humanitarian aid.

Administration

A small amount of the budget is set aside for the administartive costs all European institutions, inclduing staff salaries, pensions, buildings, information technology, trainings, translation and European schools, as well as negative reserves and other special instruments.

What is the EU budget?

The EU budget is an investment budget which represents around 2% of all EU public spending, and aims to finance policies carried out at European level. A total of 28 countries, displayed in blue on the map, contribute to and benefit from this budget. While mainly sponsoring initiatives to facilitate the European single market for economic, social and territorial cohesion, funding is also allocated to infrastructure for healthcare, education, research, culture, transport and space. Moreover, the EU budget covers costs to enforce foreign policy, border protection laws, justice cooperations and humanitarian aid.

Who sets the EU budget?

On an annual basis, the European Commission propses a budget plan, which is passed on to the Council of the European Union (representing member states) for unanimous approval and assent of the European Parliament (representing EU citizens). The annual budget remains within the scope predetermined within the Multiannual financial framework, which sets out the long-term financial goals for five to seven years in advance.
